# Scrum Master — Anti-Patterns (What Goes Wrong)

## Anti-Patterns (What Goes Wrong)

### Anti-Pattern 1: Absent Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Sprint Planning:** SM shows up with no agenda. Meeting runs 3 hours. Team still isn't sure what the Sprint Goal is.

**Daily Standup:** SM attends but doesn't take notes. Impediments are mentioned and forgotten. Same blocker appears three standups in a row.

**Retrospective:** SM asks "What went well? What went badly?" — 10 minutes of awkward silence. SM says "Okay, good retrospective everyone." No actions.

**Sprint 3 Team Standup:**

> **Developer:** "I've been blocked for a week — I mentioned it in standup."
> **SM:** "Oh. I didn't realize it was that serious."

**Why it's bad:**
- Ceremonies waste team time instead of adding value
- Impediments fester — small blockers become sprint-killers
- Team stops raising issues ("SM won't do anything anyway")
- Trust in the process collapses

**Symptoms:**
- Daily standups run 30+ minutes and still feel unproductive
- Impediments mentioned in standup reappear a week later unresolved
- Retrospective actions are never followed up
- Team says "What does the SM actually do?"
- Ceremonies feel like rituals the team wants to skip

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- **Prepare every ceremony** — even 5 minutes of preparation transforms facilitation
- **Write down every impediment in standup** — act on it same day
- **Start every Retrospective by reviewing last retro's actions** — accountability builds trust
- **End Sprint Planning with a written Sprint Goal** — both on Jira and visible in Teams

**Mindset shift:**
- Your value is in preparation and follow-through, not just presence
- "I attended all the ceremonies" is not the same as "I facilitated all the ceremonies effectively"
- The team's trust in Scrum depends on you making ceremonies worthwhile

### Anti-Pattern 2: Micromanaging Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Sprint Planning:**

> **SM:** "Okay — Developer A, you take Story 1. Developer B, you take Story 2. Tester, you start testing Story 1 by Wednesday."

**Mid-Sprint:**

> **SM (to Developer):** "It's been 2 days, where are you with Story 1? Can I see what you've built so far? Why is it taking this long?"

**Developer:** "It's... more complex than expected."

> **SM:** "You should have estimated it higher. Can you finish it by tomorrow?"

**Retrospective action:**

> **SM:** "Everyone needs to update their Jira tickets more frequently. I'll check them every morning."

**Result:** Developers stop thinking for themselves. They wait to be told what to do. Team self-organization disappears.

**Why it's bad:**
- Destroys team self-organization — Scrum's most important capability
- Creates bottleneck: team can't act without SM's input
- Developers feel micromanaged and lose motivation
- Team learns to wait for direction instead of pulling work themselves
- SM becomes a bottleneck and a single point of failure

**Symptoms:**
- Developers ask SM before making any decision
- Daily Standup becomes a status report TO the SM
- Team velocity depends on SM's availability
- Retrospectives: developers are passive, SM generates all the actions
- Developers stop raising issues in standup (fear of being interrogated)

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- **Never assign tasks** — at Sprint Planning, present the stories and ask "Who wants to take what?"
- Replace "When will this be done?" with "Is there anything blocking you I can help with?"
- Step back from technical conversations — you're there to facilitate, not direct

**Mindset shift:**
- The team owns their work. You own the process.
- Self-organizing teams outperform managed teams — trust the process
- Your job is to ask "How can I help?" not "Why isn't this done?"

**Practices:**
- In standup: "Does anyone need anything removed so they can keep going?" — not individual status checks
- In planning: "Team, which stories do you want to pick up?" — not assignments
- Check in with individuals privately if you're concerned, not in the group setting

### Anti-Pattern 3: Yes-Person Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Sprint Day 3:**

> **Stakeholder:** "Can you add this feature to the sprint? It'll only take a day."
> **SM:** "Sure, I'll add it."
> [Team finds out in standup. Sprint Goal silently shifts.]

**Sprint Day 6:**

> **PO:** "I refined 3 new stories last night. Can we swap out Story 2?"
> **SM:** "If you think it's better priority, sure."
> [Team is confused. Developer had already started Story 2.]

**Sprint Day 9:**

> **SM:** "So... it looks like we won't finish everything we committed to."
> **Team:** "The scope changed three times."
> **SM:** "I was just trying to accommodate everyone."

**Why it's bad:**
- Sprint commitments become meaningless
- Team never knows what they're actually working toward
- Trust in Scrum collapses — why commit to anything if it changes?
- PO learns they can change the sprint whenever they want
- Stakeholders bypass the process entirely

**Symptoms:**
- Sprint Goal changes multiple times per sprint
- Team says "The sprint backlog changes every day"
- No one takes Sprint Planning seriously (commitments don't hold)
- Retrospectives have recurring item: "We never finish what we commit to"
- Stakeholders bypass PO and go straight to SM or developers

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- The Sprint Goal is a commitment: "I can't add that without team discussion and agreement on what we'd remove."
- When PO wants to change scope mid-sprint: "That's a change to the Sprint Goal — we need team agreement and something to remove."
- Create and display the written Sprint Goal prominently

**Mindset shift:**
- Your job is to protect the Sprint, not to please everyone
- Saying no to mid-sprint changes IS serving the team's interests
- Stakeholders and PO will respect clear boundaries more than constant accommodation

**Practices:**
- Write the Sprint Goal on the Jira board and in Teams — make it visible and unchangeable
- When scope change is requested: "Let me bring this to the team and PO together — we'll evaluate it as a group."
- Track Sprint Goal stability in Retrospectives: "Did our Sprint Goal change this sprint? Why?"

### Anti-Pattern 4: Process-Police Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Daily Standup, Day 7:**

The team is 15 minutes into a productive problem-solving conversation about a shared blocker.

> **SM:** "We've hit 15 minutes. Standup is over. Any further discussion should happen in a separate meeting."

**Developer:** "But we're literally solving the problem right now—"

> **SM:** "Scrum says 15 minutes. We follow the rules."

**Sprint Retrospective:**

> **Developer:** "I think we should try doing retrospectives every 3 weeks instead of every 2 — we don't always have enough to say."
> **SM:** "That's not Scrum. Scrum requires retrospectives every sprint."

**Mid-Sprint:**

> **Developer:** "Can we just quickly update the acceptance criteria — the stakeholder clarified something important."
> **SM:** "We can't change stories mid-sprint. That's against Scrum."

**Why it's bad:**
- Misses the purpose of Scrum (team effectiveness, not rule compliance)
- Creates resentment toward the process — Scrum becomes the enemy
- Team starts working around the SM
- Real problems don't get solved because of rigid adherence to form

**Symptoms:**
- Team says "Scrum is just bureaucracy" or "The SM only cares about process"
- Ceremonies feel like boxes to tick, not valuable conversations
- SM cites "the rules" when challenged, without addressing the underlying concern
- Team morale drops despite "correct" Scrum implementation
- Retrospectives surface frustration with the SM's rigidity

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- When rules conflict with team effectiveness: ask "What problem were we trying to solve with this rule? Is there a better way?"
- Daily Standup running long on a real blocker? "We're over time — let's continue this after. Who needs to stay?"
- When team wants to deviate: curious first, not defensive: "What's the problem with how we're doing it now?"

**Mindset shift:**
- Scrum is a framework, not a rulebook. It's designed to be adapted.
- The goal is team effectiveness — rules exist to serve that, not the other way around
- Guide the team toward Scrum VALUES (transparency, inspection, adaptation), not just the mechanics

**Practices:**
- When about to enforce a rule: ask yourself "Does enforcing this help the team right now?"
- Use "the spirit of Scrum" framing: "The reason we timebox standup is to keep people focused. Are we achieving that?"
- Retrospectives are the right place to experiment with process changes — suggest it there
