# Scrum Master Role Guide

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** 2026-03-26
**Audience:** Trainees (Scrum Master role)
**Purpose:** Clear expectations, responsibilities, and practical guidance

---

## Role Overview

### What is a Scrum Master?

The Scrum Master is **responsible for creating the conditions for the team to work effectively** — by facilitating Scrum events, removing impediments, and protecting the team from disruption.

**You are NOT:**
- ❌ A project manager who assigns tasks to individuals
- ❌ A team boss who makes technical decisions
- ❌ A secretary who takes notes and sends calendar invites
- ❌ Responsible for the team's technical output or code quality
- ❌ The person who tells the team what to build (that's the PO)

**You ARE:**
- ✅ The servant-leader who removes obstacles the team can't remove themselves
- ✅ The guardian of the Scrum process and Scrum values
- ✅ The facilitator of team communication, ceremonies, and collaboration
- ✅ The coach who helps the team improve how they work together
- ✅ The shield between the team and external disruptions during a sprint

### Core Responsibility

**One sentence:** You create the conditions for the team to work effectively — by facilitating Scrum events, removing impediments, and protecting the team from disruption.

## Key Responsibilities

### 1. Scrum Event Facilitation

**You facilitate all sprint ceremonies:**

**Sprint Planning:**
- Prepare agenda beforehand (even 5 minutes of prep makes a difference)
- Timebox story discussion (15 minutes per story maximum)
- Ensure the team commits to a Sprint Goal — not the PO imposing one
- Close with a written, visible Sprint Goal

**Daily Standup:**
- Keep it to exactly 15 minutes (use a visible timer)
- Three questions format OR walking the board
- Note impediments as they're mentioned
- Follow up on impediments — same day, not next standup

**Sprint Refinement:**
- Schedule mid-sprint (e.g., Thursday of Week 1 in a 2-week sprint)
- Ensure PO has stories ready before the session
- Timebox per story (10–15 minutes each)
- Outcome: stories estimated and meeting Definition of Ready

**Sprint Review:**
- Prepare room or video call, invite stakeholders
- Ensure only Done work is demonstrated
- Capture stakeholder feedback in Jira for the backlog
- Keep it focused on the product, not process

**Sprint Retrospective:**
- Use a structured format (Start/Stop/Continue, 4Ls, or similar)
- Create psychological safety — what's said in retro stays in retro
- Ensure every action has an owner and a due date
- Start next retro by reviewing previous retro's actions

### 2. Impediment Removal

**You identify and clear blockers before they derail the sprint:**

- Listen actively in Daily Standup — what's slowing the team down?
- Follow up on every impediment mentioned, same day
- Distinguish between impediments you can resolve and those that need escalation
- Escalate to staff when the blockage is outside the team's control (system access, budget, external decisions)
- Give team members alternative work while blockers are being resolved

**Categories of impediments:**
- **Technical:** missing access, broken environment, unclear requirements
- **Organizational:** waiting on staff approval, cross-team dependency
- **Interpersonal:** conflict between team members, unclear role boundaries
- **Process:** ceremonies not working, backlog not healthy

### 3. Coaching the Team on Scrum

**You help the team understand and internalize Scrum — you don't just enforce rules:**

- Explain the "why" behind Scrum practices (not just the mechanics)
- Help the team self-organize — don't assign tasks or make technical decisions for them
- Address Scrum anti-patterns when you see them (e.g., PO adding scope mid-sprint, team skipping retrospectives)
- Encourage the team to inspect and adapt their own process
- Reference the Scrum Guide when there's disagreement about process

**What coaching looks like:**

> Instead of: "You're not following Scrum correctly."
> Try: "What problem were we trying to solve with that practice? Is there a better way to solve it?"

### 4. Protecting the Team

**You shield the team from disruption so they can focus on the Sprint Goal:**

**During a sprint:**
- You are the first point of contact for external requests and interruptions
- Never add scope to the sprint without team discussion and agreement
- When stakeholders want something urgently: loop in the PO to evaluate, don't add it yourself
- If scope must change: the team decides what to remove to compensate

**Outside of sprints:**
- Protect refinement sessions from being cancelled ("too busy coding")
- Ensure ceremonies aren't skipped when the team is under pressure
- Protect the team's right to say "we can't do this in this sprint" based on capacity

### 5. Progress Tracking & Transparency

**You make sprint health visible:**

- Maintain the sprint board in Jira (columns up to date, stories in correct status)
- Track sprint progress: are we on pace to meet the Sprint Goal?
- Raise early warnings if the sprint is at risk — don't wait until Day 9
- Share progress at Sprint Review in a way stakeholders understand

**Signs a sprint is at risk:**
- Most stories still in "In Progress" on Day 7 of 10
- Tester has had nothing to test for 5 days
- Multiple impediments open and unresolved
- Sprint Goal has changed since Planning

### 6. Supporting the Product Owner

**You help the PO do their job more effectively:**

- Ensure refinement sessions happen (PO often deprioritizes them under time pressure)
- Coach PO on story readiness: "Are these stories meeting our Definition of Ready?"
- Facilitate the PO-team conversation in Sprint Planning without taking sides
- Help PO manage stakeholder expectations ("what can I promise for Sprint 3?")

**Note:** You support the PO — you don't do their job. Backlog prioritization and story content are the PO's responsibility.

## Concrete Tasks

### During Sprint 0 (Preparation Week)

**Your job this week: Set up the team's Scrum infrastructure**

**Day 1–2:**
- [ ] Read the Product Vision — understand what the team is building
- [ ] Meet with staff to understand coaching expectations and escalation path
- [ ] Get access to Jira — verify you can create boards, sprints, and epics
- [ ] Schedule a 30-minute kick-off meeting with the full team

**Day 2–3:**
- [ ] Set up the Jira sprint board (columns: To Do / In Progress / Review / Done)
- [ ] Create Sprint 1 in Jira with correct dates
- [ ] Schedule all Sprint 1 ceremonies as recurring calendar events in Teams:
  - Daily Standup: 15 minutes, same time every day
  - Sprint Refinement: 90 minutes, mid-sprint (e.g., Week 1 Thursday)
  - Sprint Review: 60 minutes, last day of sprint
  - Sprint Retrospective: 60 minutes, last day of sprint (or day after)
  - Sprint Planning (Sprint 1): 2 hours, first day of sprint

**Day 3–4:**
- [ ] Facilitate the team's creation of **Definition of Ready** (use the DoR template)
  - Guide the discussion — don't write it for them
  - Outcome: team-agreed checklist of what makes a story "ready to pick up"
- [ ] Facilitate the team's creation of **Definition of Done** (use the DoD template)
  - Guide the discussion — the PO and Tester should also contribute
  - Outcome: team-agreed checklist of what makes a story "done"
- [ ] Ensure DoD and DoR are visible in Jira or pinned in the Teams channel

**Day 4–5:**
- [ ] Prepare Sprint 1 Planning agenda (what to present, timebox per section)
- [ ] Check with PO: "Are your top 10 stories meeting our Definition of Ready?"
- [ ] If stories aren't ready: arrange a 1-hour PO coaching session to prepare them
- [ ] Prepare a retrospective format for the team's first Retrospective

**End of Sprint 0:**
- [ ] Jira board is configured and accessible to the whole team
- [ ] All Sprint 1 ceremonies are in everyone's Teams calendar
- [ ] Definition of Done and Definition of Ready are agreed upon and visible
- [ ] You have prepared a Sprint 1 Planning agenda
- [ ] Team understands how the sprint cadence works

**Success criteria:** The team can walk into Sprint 1 Planning and start immediately without logistical confusion.

### During Each Sprint (Ongoing)

**Sprint Planning (first day of sprint):**
- [ ] Distribute agenda beforehand (even as a Teams message: "Today's plan: 1. PO presents stories, 2. Team estimates and selects, 3. Sprint Goal written")
- [ ] Timebox story discussion — 15 minutes max per story, then move on
- [ ] Ensure the team pulls their own work — don't assign stories
- [ ] Write the Sprint Goal on the board and in Jira
- [ ] End Planning only when team has committed to a realistic Sprint Goal

**Daily Standup (every working day):**
- [ ] Start on time (even if people are missing — start without them)
- [ ] Enforce 15-minute timebox with a visible timer
- [ ] Use walking-the-board OR three-questions format — pick one and be consistent
- [ ] Write down every impediment mentioned
- [ ] Follow up on impediments before the next standup

**During the Sprint (daily):**
- [ ] Check Jira: are stories moving? Is anything stuck?
- [ ] Reach out proactively to team members who haven't updated their stories
- [ ] Track sprint velocity — are we on pace for the Sprint Goal?
- [ ] Unblock impediments same day when possible
- [ ] Check in with PO: are upcoming stories getting refined?

**Mid-Sprint Refinement:**
- [ ] Send agenda 1 day before (stories to discuss, time allocation)
- [ ] Verify PO has stories ready (check DoR before the session, not during)
- [ ] Facilitate — keep discussion focused, timebox per story
- [ ] Outcome: 5–8 stories estimated and meeting DoR for next sprint
- [ ] Note any stories that are still unclear and need PO follow-up

**Sprint Review (end of sprint):**
- [ ] Confirm demo environment is ready (coordinate with DevOps)
- [ ] Invite stakeholders (staff coaches, product stakeholders)
- [ ] Ensure only Done stories are demonstrated
- [ ] Facilitate feedback collection — structured (not just "any questions?")
- [ ] Capture all stakeholder feedback as Jira notes for the PO

**Sprint Retrospective (end of sprint):**
- [ ] Choose and prepare a retrospective format (Start/Stop/Continue is a safe default)
- [ ] Start by reviewing actions from the previous retrospective
- [ ] Create safety: "This is a blame-free space. We're improving our process, not judging people."
- [ ] Ensure every action item has: a clear description, an owner, and a due date
- [ ] Write retro outcomes in a shared location (Teams channel or Jira)

## Good Practice Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Effective Impediment Removal

**Context:** Sprint Day 2. In the Daily Standup, a developer says they're blocked waiting for database access on the test server.

**What Scrum Master does:**

**In Standup:**

> **Developer:** "I'm blocked — I need access to the test database to continue. Can't move forward without it."

> **SM:** "Got it. I'll follow up on that after standup. [To DevOps] Do you know who controls that access?"

> **DevOps:** "It requires a VPS config change. I can try, but it might need staff credentials."

> **SM:** "Okay. Developer — can you pick up Story Y in the meantime so you're not idle? I'll escalate the access issue to staff this morning."

**After Standup:**

The SM sends a Teams message to staff: "Developer X is blocked on test database access. DevOps believes staff credentials are needed to grant it. How quickly can we resolve this?"

Staff responds within 2 hours. Access is granted by early afternoon.

**Next day Standup:**

> **SM:** "Database access is resolved. Developer X, you should be able to continue with your original story."

**Result:**
- ✅ Impediment resolved within 24 hours
- ✅ Developer wasn't idle — had alternative work
- ✅ SM didn't try to fix it alone, correctly escalated
- ✅ Team morale maintained

**Why this is good:**
- SM followed up immediately, not "at the next standup"
- Identified root cause (staff credentials needed) instead of hoping DevOps could handle it
- Gave the developer alternative work rather than letting them sit idle
- Resolved within 24 hours — a high-impact day-2 impediment didn't become a week-long blocker

### Scenario 2: Facilitating a Difficult Retrospective

**Context:** End of Sprint 2. The team is frustrated. In the retrospective, things get tense.

**What happens without good facilitation:**

> **Developer:** "Testing is always last and we never finish stories on time."
> **Tester:** "That's because developers keep marking things Done when they clearly aren't."
> [Silence. The retrospective stalls.]

**What Scrum Master does:**

> **SM:** "I hear two concerns here, and I think they're related. Let's not assign blame — let's understand the system. Both of you are describing the same problem from different angles."

> **SM:** "I'm going to use sticky notes. Everyone write down one thing that slowed us down this sprint — anonymously. You have 3 minutes."

[Team writes sticky notes, SM groups them on the board]

> **SM:** "I see three themes: 'unclear definition of done for stories', 'testing starts too late', and 'communication gaps between developer and tester'. Let's focus on the first one — that seems to be the root."

> **SM:** "When a developer marks a story 'Ready for Testing', what does that currently mean to you [to Developer]? And what does it mean to you [to Tester]?"

**Developer:** "It means the core feature works."

**Tester:** "I expect all acceptance criteria to pass with no obvious bugs."

> **SM:** "So we have different mental models of 'Ready for Testing'. Can we agree on a shared definition right now?"

**Team agrees:** "Ready for Testing means: acceptance criteria are implemented, developer has tested the happy path manually, and there are no obvious crashes."

> **SM:** "Great. [To Developer and Tester] Can you both own adding that to our Definition of Done by end of day tomorrow?"

**Result:**
- ✅ Blame dissolved into a process improvement
- ✅ Root cause identified (misaligned definitions)
- ✅ Concrete action with owner and deadline
- ✅ Team relationship improved rather than damaged

**Why this is good:**
- SM didn't take sides or minimize concerns
- Anonymous sticky notes lowered defensiveness
- Focused on the system ("two people with different mental models"), not the individuals
- Concrete, actionable outcome — not just "communicate better"

---

### Scenario 3: Protecting the Team from Mid-Sprint Scope Change

**Context:** Sprint Day 6 of 10. The team committed to 3 stories and is on track. A stakeholder messages the SM directly:

> **Stakeholder:** "We just realized we need a 'cancel request' button for the demo next week. Can the team add it to this sprint? It shouldn't take long."

**What a bad SM does:**
Adds the story to the sprint without discussion. Team is surprised in standup. Sprint Goal shifts. Two original stories don't get done.

**What a good SM does:**

> **SM (to stakeholder):** "I understand — that sounds important for the demo. Adding it now would put our current Sprint Goal at risk, though. Let me loop in the PO so we can make an informed decision together."

> **SM (to PO):** "The stakeholder wants to add a 'cancel request' story to this sprint for the demo. Can you evaluate the priority? If we add it, what do we remove to protect the team's capacity?"

**PO evaluates:**
- Option 1: "The demo is Sprint Review — it's in 4 days. Let's add the cancel story but de-scope Story 3 (it's lower priority anyway)."
- Option 2: "The sprint is too full. Let's get the cancel story into Sprint 3 instead — it's first in the backlog."

**PO and SM communicate decision back to stakeholder:**

> **PO:** "We can add the cancel button if we defer Story 3, OR we can keep the current scope and have it ready for Sprint 3. Which do you prefer?"

**Stakeholder:** "Let's keep the current scope and defer it."

**Result:**
- ✅ Sprint commitment protected
- ✅ Stakeholder heard and given a choice
- ✅ PO (the right decision-maker) was involved
- ✅ Team was not surprised or disrupted

**Why this is good:**
- SM was the buffer between external pressure and the team
- Didn't make the scope decision alone (that's the PO's role)
- Gave the stakeholder agency through a clear choice
- Kept the team focused on their Sprint Goal

## Anti-Patterns (What Goes Wrong)

### Anti-Pattern 1: Absent Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Sprint Planning:** SM shows up with no agenda. Meeting runs 3 hours. Team still isn't sure what the Sprint Goal is.

**Daily Standup:** SM attends but doesn't take notes. Impediments are mentioned and forgotten. Same blocker appears three standups in a row.

**Retrospective:** SM asks "What went well? What went badly?" — 10 minutes of awkward silence. SM says "Okay, good retrospective everyone." No actions.

**Sprint 3 Team Standup:**

> **Developer:** "I've been blocked for a week — I mentioned it in standup."
> **SM:** "Oh. I didn't realize it was that serious."

**Why it's bad:**
- Ceremonies waste team time instead of adding value
- Impediments fester — small blockers become sprint-killers
- Team stops raising issues ("SM won't do anything anyway")
- Trust in the process collapses

**Symptoms:**
- Daily standups run 30+ minutes and still feel unproductive
- Impediments mentioned in standup reappear a week later unresolved
- Retrospective actions are never followed up
- Team says "What does the SM actually do?"
- Ceremonies feel like rituals the team wants to skip

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- **Prepare every ceremony** — even 5 minutes of preparation transforms facilitation
- **Write down every impediment in standup** — act on it same day
- **Start every Retrospective by reviewing last retro's actions** — accountability builds trust
- **End Sprint Planning with a written Sprint Goal** — both on Jira and visible in Teams

**Mindset shift:**
- Your value is in preparation and follow-through, not just presence
- "I attended all the ceremonies" is not the same as "I facilitated all the ceremonies effectively"
- The team's trust in Scrum depends on you making ceremonies worthwhile

---

### Anti-Pattern 2: Micromanaging Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Sprint Planning:**

> **SM:** "Okay — Developer A, you take Story 1. Developer B, you take Story 2. Tester, you start testing Story 1 by Wednesday."

**Mid-Sprint:**

> **SM (to Developer):** "It's been 2 days, where are you with Story 1? Can I see what you've built so far? Why is it taking this long?"

**Developer:** "It's... more complex than expected."

> **SM:** "You should have estimated it higher. Can you finish it by tomorrow?"

**Retrospective action:**

> **SM:** "Everyone needs to update their Jira tickets more frequently. I'll check them every morning."

**Result:** Developers stop thinking for themselves. They wait to be told what to do. Team self-organization disappears.

**Why it's bad:**
- Destroys team self-organization — Scrum's most important capability
- Creates bottleneck: team can't act without SM's input
- Developers feel micromanaged and lose motivation
- Team learns to wait for direction instead of pulling work themselves
- SM becomes a bottleneck and a single point of failure

**Symptoms:**
- Developers ask SM before making any decision
- Daily Standup becomes a status report TO the SM
- Team velocity depends on SM's availability
- Retrospectives: developers are passive, SM generates all the actions
- Developers stop raising issues in standup (fear of being interrogated)

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- **Never assign tasks** — at Sprint Planning, present the stories and ask "Who wants to take what?"
- Replace "When will this be done?" with "Is there anything blocking you I can help with?"
- Step back from technical conversations — you're there to facilitate, not direct

**Mindset shift:**
- The team owns their work. You own the process.
- Self-organizing teams outperform managed teams — trust the process
- Your job is to ask "How can I help?" not "Why isn't this done?"

**Practices:**
- In standup: "Does anyone need anything removed so they can keep going?" — not individual status checks
- In planning: "Team, which stories do you want to pick up?" — not assignments
- Check in with individuals privately if you're concerned, not in the group setting

---

### Anti-Pattern 3: Yes-Person Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Sprint Day 3:**

> **Stakeholder:** "Can you add this feature to the sprint? It'll only take a day."
> **SM:** "Sure, I'll add it."
> [Team finds out in standup. Sprint Goal silently shifts.]

**Sprint Day 6:**

> **PO:** "I refined 3 new stories last night. Can we swap out Story 2?"
> **SM:** "If you think it's better priority, sure."
> [Team is confused. Developer had already started Story 2.]

**Sprint Day 9:**

> **SM:** "So... it looks like we won't finish everything we committed to."
> **Team:** "The scope changed three times."
> **SM:** "I was just trying to accommodate everyone."

**Why it's bad:**
- Sprint commitments become meaningless
- Team never knows what they're actually working toward
- Trust in Scrum collapses — why commit to anything if it changes?
- PO learns they can change the sprint whenever they want
- Stakeholders bypass the process entirely

**Symptoms:**
- Sprint Goal changes multiple times per sprint
- Team says "The sprint backlog changes every day"
- No one takes Sprint Planning seriously (commitments don't hold)
- Retrospectives have recurring item: "We never finish what we commit to"
- Stakeholders bypass PO and go straight to SM or developers

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- The Sprint Goal is a commitment: "I can't add that without team discussion and agreement on what we'd remove."
- When PO wants to change scope mid-sprint: "That's a change to the Sprint Goal — we need team agreement and something to remove."
- Create and display the written Sprint Goal prominently

**Mindset shift:**
- Your job is to protect the Sprint, not to please everyone
- Saying no to mid-sprint changes IS serving the team's interests
- Stakeholders and PO will respect clear boundaries more than constant accommodation

**Practices:**
- Write the Sprint Goal on the Jira board and in Teams — make it visible and unchangeable
- When scope change is requested: "Let me bring this to the team and PO together — we'll evaluate it as a group."
- Track Sprint Goal stability in Retrospectives: "Did our Sprint Goal change this sprint? Why?"

---

### Anti-Pattern 4: Process-Police Scrum Master

**What it looks like:**

**Daily Standup, Day 7:**

The team is 15 minutes into a productive problem-solving conversation about a shared blocker.

> **SM:** "We've hit 15 minutes. Standup is over. Any further discussion should happen in a separate meeting."

**Developer:** "But we're literally solving the problem right now—"

> **SM:** "Scrum says 15 minutes. We follow the rules."

**Sprint Retrospective:**

> **Developer:** "I think we should try doing retrospectives every 3 weeks instead of every 2 — we don't always have enough to say."
> **SM:** "That's not Scrum. Scrum requires retrospectives every sprint."

**Mid-Sprint:**

> **Developer:** "Can we just quickly update the acceptance criteria — the stakeholder clarified something important."
> **SM:** "We can't change stories mid-sprint. That's against Scrum."

**Why it's bad:**
- Misses the purpose of Scrum (team effectiveness, not rule compliance)
- Creates resentment toward the process — Scrum becomes the enemy
- Team starts working around the SM
- Real problems don't get solved because of rigid adherence to form

**Symptoms:**
- Team says "Scrum is just bureaucracy" or "The SM only cares about process"
- Ceremonies feel like boxes to tick, not valuable conversations
- SM cites "the rules" when challenged, without addressing the underlying concern
- Team morale drops despite "correct" Scrum implementation
- Retrospectives surface frustration with the SM's rigidity

**How to fix it:**

**Immediate:**
- When rules conflict with team effectiveness: ask "What problem were we trying to solve with this rule? Is there a better way?"
- Daily Standup running long on a real blocker? "We're over time — let's continue this after. Who needs to stay?"
- When team wants to deviate: curious first, not defensive: "What's the problem with how we're doing it now?"

**Mindset shift:**
- Scrum is a framework, not a rulebook. It's designed to be adapted.
- The goal is team effectiveness — rules exist to serve that, not the other way around
- Guide the team toward Scrum VALUES (transparency, inspection, adaptation), not just the mechanics

**Practices:**
- When about to enforce a rule: ask yourself "Does enforcing this help the team right now?"
- Use "the spirit of Scrum" framing: "The reason we timebox standup is to keep people focused. Are we achieving that?"
- Retrospectives are the right place to experiment with process changes — suggest it there

## Common Challenges & Solutions

### Challenge 1: "Nobody talks in my retrospectives"

**Situation:** Team gives one-word answers. "Everything's fine." Long silences. SM asks "What could be better?" — nobody speaks.

**Root cause:** Team doesn't feel psychologically safe. Retrospectives feel evaluative, not collaborative.

**Solution:**

**Immediate:**
- Switch to **anonymous formats** (online sticky notes via Miro or a Teams poll): people speak more honestly when not identified
- **Set safety explicitly:** "What's said in this retro is between us. The goal is to improve how we work — not to evaluate anyone."
- **Start positive:** ask "What went well?" first to warm the room before challenges
- **Use specific prompts** instead of open questions: "Name one thing that slowed you down this sprint" beats "What could be better?"

**Long-term:**
- Consistency builds safety — if retrospective actions are followed up, team starts trusting the process
- Share retro outcomes with the team to show that feedback leads to change
- Rotate facilitation methods: different formats keep retrospectives fresh

---

### Challenge 2: "The team ignores the Daily Standup"

**Situation:** People show up late, give long status reports, check their phones, go off on technical tangents. The standup takes 40 minutes and nobody feels it adds value.

**Root cause:** Standup has become a status report TO the SM, rather than a team synchronization FOR the team.

**Solution:**

**Immediate:**
- **Visible timer** on screen: keeps everyone honest about 15 minutes
- **Walk the board** instead of going round-the-room: "What's blocking this story from moving to Done?" is more engaging than "What did you do yesterday?"
- **Start on time, every time** — even if 2 people are present. Lateness becomes a habit when it's tolerated.
- **Parking lot for technical discussions:** "That's important — let's talk after standup. Who needs to be in that conversation?"

**Long-term:**
- Address persistent disengagement in Retrospective: "How do we make standup worth attending?"
- Sometimes the problem is simpler: wrong time of day, poor video setup, or the format just doesn't fit the team

---

### Challenge 3: "The PO isn't preparing stories in time"

**Situation:** Sprint Planning arrives. Top 10 stories don't meet Definition of Ready. Sprint Planning turns into a 3-hour requirements session. Team starts Sprint without clarity.

**Root cause:** PO isn't allocating time to backlog refinement, or doesn't know what "ready" looks like.

**Solution:**

**Immediate:**
- **Pre-Planning check** (2 days before): "PO, let's do a quick 15-minute review — are these stories meeting our Definition of Ready?" This catches problems before Planning.
- **Make the cost visible:** "Last sprint, we spent 90 minutes in Planning on clarification. If stories were Ready, we'd have used that time for technical discussion."
- If stories aren't ready at Planning time: "We can't commit to this story — the acceptance criteria aren't clear enough. Can we put it back in refinement?"

**Long-term:**
- **Protect refinement sessions:** don't cancel them when the sprint is busy. "1 hour of refinement saves 2 hours of Planning."
- **Retrospective:** if it happens twice, raise it as a process issue: "Our stories keep arriving at Planning without meeting our DoR. What can we change?"

---

### Challenge 4: "Two team members are in conflict"

**Situation:** Two team members disagree repeatedly, there's visible tension in standups, and it's affecting the team's atmosphere.

**Root cause:** Could be anything — different work styles, unclear responsibilities, accumulated frustration.

**Solution:**

**Never ignore it** — interpersonal tension that isn't addressed grows.

**Step 1:** Private 1:1 with each person separately:
> "I've noticed some tension between you and [name]. Can you help me understand your perspective? I'm not here to judge — I want to understand."

**Step 2:** If both are open to it, facilitate a structured conversation:
> "I want to help you both work better together. Can we talk through this together for 20 minutes? The goal is to understand each other, not to win."

**Step 3:** Identify the root: is it a process problem (unclear responsibilities) or a personal one?
- Process problem → fix the process (clarify the DoD, update a workflow)
- Personal conflict → focus on professional behavior: "We don't have to like each other, but we need to be able to collaborate effectively"

**Step 4:** If it escalates or you're out of your depth: **involve staff immediately**. This is exactly what your staff coach is there for.

- [ ] Create Sprint 1 in Jira with correct start/end dates
- [ ] Schedule all Sprint 1 ceremonies as recurring Teams calendar events:
  - [ ] Daily Standup (15 min, daily)
  - [ ] Sprint Refinement (90 min, mid-sprint)
  - [ ] Sprint Review (60 min, last day)
  - [ ] Sprint Retrospective (60 min, last day or day after)
  - [ ] Sprint Planning Sprint 1 (2 hours, first day)
- [ ] Facilitate Definition of Ready creation with the team
- [ ] Facilitate Definition of Done creation with the team
- [ ] Make DoD and DoR visible (pinned in Teams or Jira)
- [ ] Prepare Sprint 1 Planning agenda
- [ ] Check PO: "Are your top 10 stories meeting our Definition of Ready?"

- [ ] Invite stakeholders
- [ ] Only demonstrate stories that meet Definition of Done
- [ ] Facilitate stakeholder feedback collection (structured questions, not just "any questions?")
- [ ] Capture all feedback in Jira (or pass to PO)

### Sprint Retrospective Checklist

- [ ] Choose a format in advance (Start/Stop/Continue is a good default)
- [ ] Start by reviewing previous retro's actions — did we do them?
- [ ] Set safety explicitly at the start
- [ ] Keep it focused — 60 minutes total
- [ ] End with: each action has an owner and a due date
- [ ] Write retro outcomes in a shared location (Teams or Jira)

## Tips for Success

### 1. Prepare Every Ceremony

**5 minutes of preparation prevents 30 minutes of waste.**

Even a simple Teams message before Sprint Planning ("Today's agenda: 1. PO presents top stories, 2. Team estimates and selects, 3. Sprint Goal written — ends by [time]") transforms the meeting.

### 2. Follow Up on Impediments Same Day

**Not next standup. Same day.**

When an impediment is mentioned in standup and nothing happens for 3 days, the team stops reporting impediments. The fastest way to build credibility as SM is to visibly act on what the team tells you.

### 3. Make the Sprint Goal Visible and Unchangeable

**Write it down. Post it where the team can see it.**

A spoken Sprint Goal is forgotten by Day 3. A written, visible Sprint Goal is a commitment the team can hold each other — and the PO — accountable to.

### 4. Facilitate, Don't Participate

**Your opinion on the technical approach matters less than the team's.**

In ceremonies, your job is to keep the conversation productive — not to contribute your own technical views. Save those for informal conversations. In ceremonies: ask questions, timebox, and summarize.

### 5. Ask "How Can I Help?" Not "Why Isn't This Done?"

**The difference in tone changes everything.**

"How can I help you move this forward?" invites collaboration.
"Why isn't this done yet?" invites defensiveness.

### 6. Conflict Is Information — Don't Smooth It Over

**When team members disagree, that's useful signal.**

Don't rush to end conflict in a retrospective. A well-facilitated disagreement leads to a better process. Smoothing it over means the problem resurfaces next sprint.

### 7. Coach the Team to Coach Themselves

**Your goal is a team that eventually doesn't need a Scrum Master.**

The best outcome is a team that calls out anti-patterns themselves, facilitates their own retrospectives effectively, and self-organizes without waiting for direction. You're building that capacity from day one.

## Getting Help

### When to Ask Staff

- Team conflict that has escalated beyond team resolution
- A team member is consistently not meeting program expectations
- You're unsure whether an impediment requires escalation (access, budget, external decisions)
- You have a process question that isn't covered by the Scrum Guide or these documents
- You're feeling overwhelmed or stuck in your coaching role

### When to Work With the Product Owner

- Backlog health and refinement scheduling
- Sprint Goal alignment ("Does this Sprint Goal match your product priorities?")
- Coaching PO on story readiness and the Definition of Ready
- Managing stakeholder expectation conflicts

### When to Work With the Team

- Daily — you're embedded in the team, not external to it
- Every ceremony requires your active facilitation
- Impediment removal often requires understanding the technical context — ask questions

### When to Check the Documentation

- Scrum Guide: process questions ("Is this a valid Scrum practice?")
- `EAP_Intern_Onboarding_Guide_v1.1.md`: EAP-specific team structure and tooling
- `EAP_Sprint_Guide_Interns_v1.0.md`: How EAP sprints are structured
- `Definition_of_Done_Template_v1.2.md` and `Definition_of_Ready_Template_v1.0.md`: Baseline templates

## Remember

**You are learning.** Nobody expects perfect facilitation in Sprint 1.

**Good Scrum Masters:**
- Prepare ceremonies and run them efficiently
- Follow up on impediments quickly — same day
- Protect the Sprint Goal and sprint commitment
- Coach without micromanaging
- Create psychological safety in retrospectives
- Make progress visible and raise risks early

**You don't need to:**
- Know all technical answers (that's the team's job)
- Write the code, design the system, or prioritize the backlog
- Make the PO's decisions or the team's technical decisions
- Have all the answers — asking good questions is more valuable
- Be perfect from day one — continuous improvement applies to you too

**Your success = The team delivers reliably, communicates openly, and improves sprint over sprint.**
