    Scrum Master — Role Overview

    Role Overview

    What is a Scrum Master?

    The Scrum Master is responsible for creating the conditions for the team to work effectively — by facilitating Scrum events, removing impediments, and protecting the team from disruption.

    You are NOT: - ❌ A project manager who assigns tasks to individuals - ❌ A team boss who makes technical decisions - ❌ A secretary who takes notes and sends calendar invites - ❌ Responsible for the team's technical output or code quality - ❌ The person who tells the team what to build (that's the PO)

    You ARE: - ✅ The servant-leader who removes obstacles the team can't remove themselves - ✅ The guardian of the Scrum process and Scrum values - ✅ The facilitator of team communication, ceremonies, and collaboration - ✅ The coach who helps the team improve how they work together - ✅ The shield between the team and external disruptions during a sprint

    Core Responsibility

    One sentence: You create the conditions for the team to work effectively — by facilitating Scrum events, removing impediments, and protecting the team from disruption.

    Key Responsibilities

    1. Scrum Event Facilitation

    You facilitate all sprint ceremonies:

    Sprint Planning: - Prepare agenda beforehand (even 5 minutes of prep makes a difference) - Timebox story discussion (15 minutes per story maximum) - Ensure the team commits to a Sprint Goal — not the PO imposing one - Close with a written, visible Sprint Goal

    Daily Standup: - Keep it to exactly 15 minutes (use a visible timer) - Three questions format OR walking the board - Note impediments as they're mentioned - Follow up on impediments — same day, not next standup

    Sprint Refinement: - Schedule mid-sprint (e.g., Thursday of Week 1 in a 2-week sprint) - Ensure PO has stories ready before the session - Timebox per story (10–15 minutes each) - Outcome: stories estimated and meeting Definition of Ready

    Sprint Review: - Prepare room or video call, invite stakeholders - Ensure only Done work is demonstrated - Capture stakeholder feedback in Jira for the backlog - Keep it focused on the product, not process

    Sprint Retrospective: - Use a structured format (Start/Stop/Continue, 4Ls, or similar) - Create psychological safety — what's said in retro stays in retro - Ensure every action has an owner and a due date - Start next retro by reviewing previous retro's actions

    2. Impediment Removal

    You identify and clear blockers before they derail the sprint:

    • Listen actively in Daily Standup — what's slowing the team down?
    • Follow up on every impediment mentioned, same day
    • Distinguish between impediments you can resolve and those that need escalation
    • Escalate to staff when the blockage is outside the team's control (system access, budget, external decisions)
    • Give team members alternative work while blockers are being resolved

    Categories of impediments: - Technical: missing access, broken environment, unclear requirements - Organizational: waiting on staff approval, cross-team dependency - Interpersonal: conflict between team members, unclear role boundaries - Process: ceremonies not working, backlog not healthy

    3. Coaching the Team on Scrum

    You help the team understand and internalize Scrum — you don't just enforce rules:

    • Explain the "why" behind Scrum practices (not just the mechanics)
    • Help the team self-organize — don't assign tasks or make technical decisions for them
    • Address Scrum anti-patterns when you see them (e.g., PO adding scope mid-sprint, team skipping retrospectives)
    • Encourage the team to inspect and adapt their own process
    • Reference the Scrum Guide when there's disagreement about process

    What coaching looks like:

    Instead of: "You're not following Scrum correctly." Try: "What problem were we trying to solve with that practice? Is there a better way to solve it?"

    4. Protecting the Team

    You shield the team from disruption so they can focus on the Sprint Goal:

    During a sprint: - You are the first point of contact for external requests and interruptions - Never add scope to the sprint without team discussion and agreement - When stakeholders want something urgently: loop in the PO to evaluate, don't add it yourself - If scope must change: the team decides what to remove to compensate

    Outside of sprints: - Protect refinement sessions from being cancelled ("too busy coding") - Ensure ceremonies aren't skipped when the team is under pressure - Protect the team's right to say "we can't do this in this sprint" based on capacity

    5. Progress Tracking & Transparency

    You make sprint health visible:

    • Maintain the sprint board in Jira (columns up to date, stories in correct status)
    • Track sprint progress: are we on pace to meet the Sprint Goal?
    • Raise early warnings if the sprint is at risk — don't wait until Day 9
    • Share progress at Sprint Review in a way stakeholders understand

    Signs a sprint is at risk: - Most stories still in "In Progress" on Day 7 of 10 - Tester has had nothing to test for 5 days - Multiple impediments open and unresolved - Sprint Goal has changed since Planning

    6. Supporting the Product Owner

    You help the PO do their job more effectively:

    • Ensure refinement sessions happen (PO often deprioritizes them under time pressure)
    • Coach PO on story readiness: "Are these stories meeting our Definition of Ready?"
    • Facilitate the PO-team conversation in Sprint Planning without taking sides
    • Help PO manage stakeholder expectations ("what can I promise for Sprint 3?")

    Note: You support the PO — you don't do their job. Backlog prioritization and story content are the PO's responsibility.
